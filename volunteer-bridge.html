Volunteer Bridge: Leveling the Playing Field for Elders

MY ROLE: User Research, Personas, Ideation, Storytelling, Strategy  ||  TEAM: Sanjana Mathur, Charlie Mahoney, Ujala Qasim, Meina Zhu  ||  DURATION: 3 Weeks

CHALLENGE

Our prompt was based off the CHI 2016 design brief: Leveling The Playing Field. We were asked to explore user groups that are usually overlooked or excluded in design and technology. My team and I decided to focus on isolated elders. Especially after retirement, the elderly often feel isolated from the community. Many still desire to contribute to the community and connect with similar people.

The elderly receive far less attention from designers and technologists. Our challenge is to create a design that addresses the emotional needs of isolated elders.

Target Group: Retired individuals age 65+​ with ​no immediate family​ ​or support group​ and who live on their own or in a retirement home.

PROCESS

  

RESEARCH

PRIMARY RESEARCH

Our research centered around identifying the lifestyles of isolated elderly and their painpoints. We interviewed not only the elderly population, but also those that work with the elderly (hospital counselor, firefighters, academic researcher, and a social worker).

To get in touch with the elderly population, we visited their homes and senior care facilties during social hours. During our research, we did not ask the elderly direct questions regarding isolation and lonliness. We felt asking about a personal topic would be intrusive, and participants might feel uncomfortable opening up or admitting the realities of their condition(s). Instead, we opted to approach our research with the lens of having an open conversation about their histories, lifestyles, and desires.

IDEATION

Objective: Create a design that fulfills the desires of isolated elderly individuals.

To begin the ideation process, we reviewed our research and then parted ways to sketch ideas individually. Some of our ideas included: ambience (creating a feeling that someone is there through an artifact), storytelling, interactive games, and community involvement.

Discussing our sketches

Final Concept: An app (for desktop or tablet) that connects online volunteering opportunities to elders. The elderly can complete volunteering through the app itself. We chose this concept because it address the desires and painpoints of elderly that we found in our research. Bringing volunteer opportunities eliminates the issue of transportation (requesting transportation also makes the elderly feel like a burden), and elders can fulfill their desire to give back. The app should also allow elders to connect with other volunteers with similar interests using a social network.

Rough Concept

  

DESIGN

There currently exists a plethora of virtual volunteering opportunities on the web, many which can be taken up by the elderly. However, the elderly don’t have the technical knowledge to search the web for these opportunities and complete them. Volunteer Bridge gives them these opportunities to connect and contribute directly to society.

STRATEGY

PLACEMENT

  • Acitivities directors and staff at senior care facilities can incorporate Volunteer Bridge as an activity.
  • The same secondary user group can introduce the app to elders.
  • Volunteer Bridge can be more than an app - it could take the form of an affordable tablet (like a Kindle). Those without a tablet will be able to purchase a Volunteer Bridge tablet and use it in their home.

LIMITATIONS

  • Requires basic computer literacy.
  • Learning algorithm will be slow at first to refine results to best match the user's interests.
  • A research study revealed that the children of elderly are the ones to introduce them to virtual volunteering. Our target group may not have contact with a younger generation to introduce them to the app.

FUTURE CONSIDERATIONS

  • Reward: Organizations who received help will be encouraged to send a “Thank you” message back to elderly to reinforce the idea of strengthening connections.
  • Reward: Add a recognition prize after the user has completed a milestone. Rewards could help elderly feel a sense of accomplishment.
  • Expansion: Allow local social service organizations like women’s shelters, university departments, etc. to add volunteering tasks. This will foster a sense of community involvement.

What I Learned

1. When doing research, don't overlook what's already working.

When going into research, we look for problems to solve. I went into our primary research assuming our target group would have more problems than advantages to discuss. It turned out to they had lots of positives to say about their lifestyles and living conditions. For our design, we were able to solve for problems and build off positives we found in our research.

1. When doing research, don't overlook what's already working.

When going into research, we look for problems to solve. I went into our primary research assuming our target group would have more problems than advantages to discuss. It turned out to they had lots of positives to say about their lifestyles and living conditions. For our design, we were able to solve for problems and build off positives we found in our research.

2. Be conscious of creating the right design, for the wrong problem.

Ultimately, our design did not solve our core issue: isolation in elders. We had the worked towards the idea of fullfillment and connectedness, but this tool alone won't solve isolation. It would have helped to have a checkpoint every step in the process to evaluate if our ideas and design circle back to the core issue.

3. Incorporate incentive for users to come back.

What will keep users returning to your technology? A sense of accomplishment, a reward, easy access with customer support? Intially, we received critique that our design contains no incentive to keep users coming back. To remedy this, the user is shown where and to whom they made a difference for a sense of accomplishment. Small experiences like these can add up to a holistic and satisfying user experience.
