Assurance in Self-Driving RV’s

MY ROLE: Sketching, Ideation, Interaction Design, Usability Testing  ||  TEAM: Diandian Cao, Tosh Anand, Braden King  ||  DURATION: 1 Week  ||  TOOLS: Sketch, Photoshop, InVision, Balsamiq

CHALLENGE

The concept of self-driving cars is becoming a reality. We were asked to pick an autonomous vehicle type and take into account factors such as safety, trust, control, and efficiency, and then come up with a set of imaginative designs that speak to these new vehicles and address their issues. For this project, my team decided to focus on projected issues that may arise in self-driving RV’s.

Target Group: RV Drivers & Owners

PROCESS

  

RESEARCH

*For this project, one person was asked to switch from their original team to another team. Upon being onboarded into another group and design focus, it was my responsibility to get caught up with the literature surrounding RV’s, my group’s research insights, their design process, and team dynamics. This primary research was conducted before I joined this group. When I arrived, we used this research to define a problem space.

My team began with a preliminary round of research to identify motivations of RV owners. They interviewed RV owners, campground staff, and RV salesman. They engaged in open-ended conversations about user’s motivations and experiences with RV's and encouraged participants to share stories from previous trips. The goal for primary research was to peel back layers to identify why people buy and drive RV's.

RESEARCH FINDINGS

  • Family time is the main motivation.
  • Presented with the concept of being driven by a chauffeur, drivers expressed that they would still worry about surroundings.
  • When presented with the idea of self-driving RV's, users predicted they still might not trust a self-driving RV.

INSIGHTS TO DRIVE DESIGN

  • Autonomous RV users won't feel safe when someone else is in control or driving.
  • While experiencing the self-driving RV, users will still have the cognitive burden of worrying about safety which will take user away from their present moment.

PROBLEM SPACE

  • Provide assurance to "driver"/owner that the RV is safe while on the road.
CORE: ”People want to spend quality family time on their vacations.”
  

IDEATION

Objective: Provide a tool that provides the "driver" (person in charge) assurance, without distracting them from family time.

Concept: A ubiquitous technology that blends into the RV’s environment while still providing assurance and control directly from the interface.

  

CONCEPT TESTING

My team and I approached three RV drivers for our concept testing. We firstly described the context of self-driving cars and RV’s in the future and then explained the concept of a view/control interface for assurance. We presented rough sketches of both the screen and mobile version for users to evaluate which platform would work best with their needs.

FEEDBACK

  • Drivers pointed out that they might initially be tempted to keep glancing at the screen, but would eventually “forget about it.”
  • “The more comfortable I am, the farther away I get from it.”

FINDINGS

  • There are two types of drivers:
    1. Desire full control of the interface (don’t want anyone else to have access to it, prefers the mobile platform)
    2. Ok with relinquishing control to other passengers (other family members/friend, prefers the embedded platform)
  • There are specific road conditions that users want to be alerted about

ACTIONS FOR DESIGN

  • Address both types of drivers through providing users with options of both an embedded interface and a mobile device.
  • Add control options and task flows for specified road conditions.

DESIGN

We narrowed down to creating four specific road alerts that users said they'd like to see:

1. Traffic views (proximity to other vehicles)

2. Utility levels (gas, fluids)

3. Weather (rain, snow)

4. Speed

For this project, we were constrained for time. For this reason we designed control options and interactions only for the weather alert. Our concept testing revealed what users would do in hazardous weather conditions. We created flows based on these two options:

  1. Pull Over - The user can request the autonomous RV to pull over until hazardous weather conditions pass.
  2. Go To - One of the closest locations to the RV. Passengers in the RV can stop at one of these locations until hazardous weather conditions pass. Once the location is selected, the RV drives passengers towards that location.

Users also have the capability to take control and drive the autonomous RV if they feel the need to.

  

USABILITY TESTING

We conducted our usability test on a male who has been a passenger in a self-driving Tesla multiple times, and contrarily an undergraduate female who claims she is not tech savvy. We first provided the context of self-driving RV’s to users, and then the context of where the embedded system would fit into an RV. Through a paper prototype, each user was provided with a task to complete upon entering into the weather alert. When the screen changed through an interaction, the user was asked to reflect on what they thought just happened.

For each user, we recorded their responses/painpoints for each task. After the usability test, we went back and asked both participants how they think each painpoint could be redesigned so that it was more usable. Taking the user’s suggestions into consideration, we translated each painpoint to an actionable item for redesign.

RESULTS + ACTIONS FOR REDESIGN

  • 'Go To' function is unclear to users → add logos to signify this takes you to the closest locations
  • 'Take Control' memo is confusing → add a steering wheel icon to show driver can also take control of steering wheel if needed
  • Time, distance, and progress bar en route to McDonald's is not noticeable → make these features more defined

What I Learned

1. Communicate through sketching.

There were moments where it was difficult for us as a team to express our individual ideas. Sometimes ideas were undermined because they were not communicated clearly. We made it a habit to always be sketching our thoughts or drawing on a whiteboard. Consequently, our process was more efficient and our thoughts were expressed clearly.

2. Translate insights into actionable design.

It helped to create a chart that lists research insights in one column, and in another column list how those insights can be incorporated into our design. This ensures the design is molded by research, and that every design decision is backed with a rationale.

3. Ask users for suggestions.

Through both our concept testing and usability testing, we asked users for recommendations of features they’d want to see and ideas for building a user-friendly interface. Getting feedback from participants took out a lot of ‘guessing’ what would be best for the end-user.

4. Don't take all research findings for face value.

In our usability testing, we found our user's painpoints and suggestions for improvements towards usability. We quickly updated our design to reflect their suggestions. In hindsight, we could have questioned the 'why' behind our insights, why the user suggested what they did, if their suggestions fit into our design, and if there are other options to improve usability in that area. Before acting on research, question the results.
